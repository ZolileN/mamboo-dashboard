# Example portfolio insights

## Executive story
- Revenue is concentrated in Storage and Cleaning, which makes them the most important categories for availability protection.
- A relatively small share of SKUs drives the majority of revenue, supporting ABC-style assortment prioritisation.
- Stockout pressure matters most in high-volume stores where missed availability likely translates into lost sales, not just delayed purchases.

## Store-level talking points
- Flagship and major mall stores should be measured on both revenue scale and stockout pressure.
- Low-margin stores with high discount dependence may need pricing, mix, or replenishment intervention rather than more promotions.
- Branch comparisons are more useful when read alongside category mix and inventory cover, not as raw sales rankings alone.

## Inventory talking points
- Cover days below 7 indicate replenishment urgency; cover above 60 can indicate trapped cash.
- Reorder points and supplier lead times help distinguish short-term noise from real availability risk.
- Inter-store transfers are most attractive where one branch has excess cover and another has repeated stockout pressure on the same SKU.

## Promotion talking points
- Promotions should be evaluated on gross profit, not just top-line revenue.
- Small discounts can still be worthwhile if they increase units without materially eroding margin.
- Category response to discounting is uneven, so campaign design should reflect category economics.

## Good recommendations to say out loud in a demo
- Protect top A-class SKUs in high-revenue stores with tighter replenishment thresholds.
- Review heavy discounting in categories where unit growth is not converting into profit growth.
- Use stock transfers before emergency replenishment where excess inventory already exists elsewhere.
- Rationalise low-revenue tail SKUs if they also show weak margin and low velocity.
